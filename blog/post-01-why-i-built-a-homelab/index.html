Why I Built a Homelab (and What I'm Actually Building)

8 April 2026

Tags: homelab, devops, proxmox, kubernetes, career

I've been working in tech for a while, and there's a recurring gap I kept running into: I could talk about Kubernetes, Terraform, and HA clusters in interviews, but I hadn't actually operated them under real conditions. So I decided to fix that by building a homelab that wouldn't let me cheat — production-like constraints, real failure modes, and everything documented.

The Problem With "Learning Kubernetes" Online

You can spin up a local k3s cluster on your laptop in about 10 minutes. I did that. And then I ran the tutorial, deployed a "hello world," and... didn't really learn anything. The hard parts of operating Kubernetes — HA control planes, etcd backups, node failures, ingress with real TLS, GitOps workflows — those don't show up in a three-node local cluster that disappears when you close your laptop.

What I actually wanted to learn was the stuff that matters when you're on call: What happens when a control plane node goes down? Where does your state live? Is your cluster actually highly available, or just labeled highly available? You can't learn that from a blog post. You have to break things.

So I decided to build a homelab that mirrors how I'd set up infrastructure at work — with proper HA, network segmentation, GitOps, and observability. Not a media server. Not a NAS. Infrastructure.

The Hardware

The good news: I already had most of the hardware sitting in a closet. Two Intel NUC 11 i7s that I'd picked up over the years, and a Synology DS920+ NAS I'd been using for backups.

pve01: NUC11PAHi7 — Intel i7-1165G7, 64GB DDR4, 465GB NVMe
pve02: NUC11TNKi7 — Intel i7-1165G7, 64GB DDR4, 500GB NVMe
Storage: Synology DS920+ — 7TB usable over NFS

The NUCs are identical in CPU and RAM (i7-1165G7, 64GB), which matters a lot for Proxmox — you need CPU architecture compatibility to live-migrate VMs between nodes. Two NUCs with different CPU generations would be a problem. Two NUCs with the same generation is a free pass for live migration.

Phase 1 cost: $0. Everything came out of existing inventory. That felt like an important constraint to set upfront — this project had to work with what I had, not require a shopping spree.

The Plan

I mapped out the build in five phases:

  1. Phase 1 — Hardware & Proxmox: Get a real HA hypervisor cluster running. Not just two servers — an actual cluster with quorum and failover.
  2. Phase 2 — Networking: VLAN segmentation, a dedicated lab network, proper DNS. No "just put everything on the main network."
  3. Phase 3 — DevOps Toolchain: k3s Kubernetes cluster, provisioned with OpenTofu (IaC), configured with Ansible, deployed via Argo CD (GitOps).
  4. Phase 4 — SRE & Observability: Prometheus, Grafana, SLO definitions, runbooks, incident log. Treat it like a real platform.
  5. Phase 5 — Governance & Reliability: Close the known gaps: 3rd etcd node, off-site backups, CI/CD for infrastructure, secrets hygiene.

The guiding principle is learn by doing in production-like conditions. If I wouldn't accept a corner-cut at work, I don't cut it here either.

Why Document Everything?

Two reasons.

The first is practical: I work with AI coding assistants (Claude, Gemini) as engineering collaborators. Keeping a clean, structured repository means the AI has accurate context when I pick the project back up after a week off. Every non-obvious decision gets an Architecture Decision Record (ADR) — a short doc explaining what I chose and why. That discipline keeps future-me (and future-AI) from second-guessing or re-litigating decisions that are already settled.

The second reason is this blog. I want a public record of what I built and how I thought about it. Not a polished "look how smart I am" retrospective, but a real log of the trade-offs, the things that didn't work, and the decisions I'd make differently. That's more valuable to anyone reading than another "here's how to install k3s" post.

What This Taught Me

Before I started building, I thought the hard part of a homelab would be the hardware and the configuration. It turns out the hard part is the decisions — and the discipline to document them before you forget why you made them. Writing an ADR right after you make a choice, when the context is fresh, is completely different from trying to reconstruct your reasoning six months later.
